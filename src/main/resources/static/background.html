Satellites!

Background Information

Formulas, summaries, and more!

What are the different kinds of satellites? There are 2 main types of satellites: man-made and natural. Satellites are not always man-made, they can be celestial bodies that revolve around another bodys. (Example: the moon revolves around the Earth). Man made satellites can be classified by their function. There are several kinds: communications, remote sensing, navigation, drone, ground, polar, and others. Communications satellites recieve data and relay them back to Earth, for our use. Remote sensing satellites collect data about a specific object.

Satellite Orbit: Satellites orbit the Earth, and other celestial bodies, in a range of shapes. The orbit can range from a perfect circle to a long, flat ellipse. Knowing the equations of ellipses can be useful in visualizing a satellite's orbit.

What are the keplerian elements? Orbital elements are the parameters required to uniquely identify a specific orbit. In celestial mechanics these elements are considered in two-body systems using a Kepler orbit.

Keplerian Elements:
  • Epoch Time-A timestamp at which to measure when the orbital elements were recorded.
  • Orbital Inclination-Inclination is the angle of the orbit in relation to Earth's equator.
  • Right Ascension of the Right Node-The ascending node is the place where the satellite crosses the equator while going from the Southern Hemisphere to the Northern Hemisphere. Now since the Earth rotates, you need to specify a fixed object in space. We use Aries (this is also the same location as the vernal equinox). The angle, from the center of the Earth, between Aries and the ascending node is called the right ascension of ascending node.
  • Eccentricity-Orbital eccentricity of an astronomical object is a dimensionless parameter that determines the amount by which its orbit around another body deviates from a perfect circle.
  • Argument of Perigee-The point where the satellite is the closest to the Earth is called the perigee. The argument of perigee is the angle formed between the perigee and the ascending node. If the perigee would occur at the ascending node, the argument of perigee would be 0.
  • Mean Motion-Mean Motion is defined as the number of orbits the satellite completes about the Earth in exactly 24 hours (one solar day).
  • Mean Anomaly-Mean anomaly is the fraction of an elliptical orbit's period that has elapsed since the orbiting body passed periapsis, expressed as an angle.
  • Drag-If the satellite were in a low orbit, then the atmosphere would produce drag. This would cause the satellite to come closer to the Earth therefore speeding up (Kepler's Law).
